### 🏛️ Lock-In and the Revolutionary Challenge

The phrase means that a country's deep economic rules—protections for investors, independent central banks, privatized industries—are enshrined in international treaties and contracts. A new government with a radical mandate cannot simply change them by passing a new law. These rules are "locked in" at the international level.

A revolution is the ultimate stress test for this system. What happens when a new government declares, "These debts were incurred by a corrupt dictator and we will not pay them," or "We are nationalizing foreign-owned industries for the public good"?

The system responds through three primary mechanisms, which function like a **carrot, stick, and cage**:

### 📜 The Power of "State Succession": A Historical Cage

This third point—the doctrine of state succession—is the legal bedrock of lock-in. It is the principle that a state's obligations survive a change in government . The legal personality of the state is considered continuous. From this perspective, the Bolsheviks in 1917 or the Sandinistas in 1979 were not new entities; they were simply the new managers of the same old "company" (the state), and thus responsible for all its previous debts and treaties .

However, history shows that this cage is not unbreakable. The question is not whether a revolution can break free, but at what cost. Here are two contrasting historical examples:

- **Revolutionary Russia (1917) - The Costly Break**: The Bolsheviks, upon seizing power, issued a Decree on Peace which explicitly repudiated all Tsarist-era foreign debts, calling them "loans concluded by the tsarist government... undoubtedly burdened with signatures of the former Russian landowners and capitalists" . The response from the international community and capital markets was immediate and severe. Russia was effectively locked out of global finance for decades. It became a pariah state in the Western financial system, a status that persisted in many ways until the fall of the Soviet Union. They broke the cage, but the price was near-total economic isolation.
- **Costa Rica (1923) - The Path to Acceptance**: In a fascinating historical episode, Costa Rica's dictator, Federico Tinoco, took out loans and then was overthrown. His successor refused to repay the debt, arguing it was an illegitimate obligation of a corrupt regime. The case went to arbitration before the Chief Justice of the U.S. Supreme Court. The arbitrator ruled _against_ Costa Rica, establishing a powerful precedent that a change in government does not affect the state's obligation to pay its debts, regardless of the character of the prior regime . This ruling became a cornerstone of the "debt continuity" doctrine.

These examples illustrate the two paths: a revolutionary break that leads to financial exile, or acceptance of the old rules as the price of continued participation in the global economy. The system is built to make the second path seem like the only rational choice.

The ultimate goal of this architecture is to make a genuine, systemic revolution that challenges the primacy of capital not just politically difficult, but existentially threatening to the state's economic survival. It forces a revolutionary government to choose between its mandate and its solvency.

### The Chain Reaction: From Seizure to Repression

Your scenario sets off a cascade of consequences, moving from the international legal sphere to the streets.

**Step 1: The Expropriation**
Workers seize and occupy a factory owned by a corporation from, say, Germany or the United States. From the perspective of international investment law, this is a direct and illegal expropriation of property. The fact that it's done by workers, not the state, is crucial. The state is now on notice.

**Step 2: The State's Dilemma**
The national government is now caught between two forces. Domestically, it faces a revolutionary movement it may or may not support. Internationally, it faces an inevitable legal assault. The state has two main choices, both with severe consequences.

**Step 3: The ISDS Trigger (The "Brutal Repression" Path)**
If the state does nothing to immediately restore the factory to its foreign owner, it is in breach of its treaty obligations. The foreign corporation will file an ISDS claim, suing the state for the full value of the factory, plus lost profits.

- **The Claim:** The corporation argues the state failed to provide "full protection and security," a standard clause in virtually all BITs. This clause explicitly requires the state to protect foreign investments from physical seizure, whether by state actors or private parties like striking workers.
- **The Cost:** The potential award could be in the hundreds of millions or billions of dollars—a sum that could bankrupt the state.
- **The Result:** To avoid this catastrophic financial penalty, the state faces overwhelming pressure from international capital, its own treasury, and allied foreign governments to act. The most direct and "effective" action is to send in police or military forces to evict the workers, arrest the leaders, and return the factory to the corporation. This is the "brutal repression" your question posits.

**Step 4: The Financial Strangulation Path**
Even if the state somehow resists the immediate pressure to repress, the financial system delivers another blow. As we discussed, credit rating agencies would likely downgrade the state's debt to "junk" status, citing the "expropriation" as an extreme risk for all foreign capital. The state would be locked out of international loans, its currency would plummet, and the resulting economic chaos would create its own pressures for a brutal crackdown to "restore order" and "investor confidence."

### Why the System is Built This Way

This isn't a bug; it's a feature. The system is designed to make the cost of challenging property rights so high that no rational government would ever allow it. The "full protection and security" clause is the linchpin. It transforms the state into an insurer and enforcer for foreign capital. The state's sovereignty is not just limited; it is actively _mobilized_ to protect foreign investment from its own population.

This dynamic is precisely what critics mean when they say ISDS creates a "regulatory chill" that extends to the most fundamental social conflicts. It doesn't just discourage new laws; it mandates the active suppression of direct action that threatens property.
