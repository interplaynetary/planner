# Two Transitions: A Guided Story

_A narrative guide through the transition framework in spec.md, told through two concrete scopes — one in a high-income country, one in a low-income country. The same variables apply to both; the numbers reveal why the transition unfolds so differently._

---

## The Two Scopes

### Scope A — Rheinmetall Precision, Dortmund (Germany)

A CNC machining plant producing suspension components — precision-forged aluminium parts — for BMW, Volkswagen, and Mercedes-Benz. 200 workers. Founded 1963. Owned by a family holding company that also controls two other metalworking facilities.

**Starting conditions (Month 0):**

```
dollar_wage (average)             = €3,500 / month  (€21.20 / hour, 165 hours)
uncovered_essential_expenses:
    housing (rent or mortgage)    = €900
    childcare (private)           = €450  (for workers with children, ~60%)
    dental / prescriptions        = €100
    total                         ≈ €1,450 / month

effective_net_market_wage         ≈ €2,050 / month  (59% of dollar_wage is discretionary)

social_input_fraction             = 0
social_output_fraction            = 0
confirmed_output_fraction         = 0
communal_need_coverage[i]         = 0
communal_satisfaction_ratio       = undefined (denominator is effective_net_market_wage)
```

Workers are materially comfortable by global standards but deeply tethered: the mortgage and the childcare bill together consume 40% of the wage before food is bought. A wage cut of 20% is catastrophic, not inconvenient. This will matter when `social_output_fraction` climbs and `market_revenue` falls.

---

### Scope B — Horizon Garments, Chittagong (Bangladesh)

An export textile mill producing T-shirts and casual wear for H&M, Zara, and Primark. 800 workers, 680 of them women. The factory is eight storeys, built in 2008, and produces roughly 12 million garments per year. Owned by a domestic conglomerate with ties to the governing party.

**Starting conditions (Month 0):**

```
dollar_wage (average)             = $150 / month  ($0.89 / hour, 168 hours — legal minimum)
uncovered_essential_expenses:
    rent (slum / company housing) = $35
    food                          = $45
    children's schooling          = $20
    out-of-pocket healthcare      = $15
    total                         ≈ $115 / month

effective_net_market_wage         ≈ $35 / month  (only 23% of dollar_wage is discretionary)

social_input_fraction             = 0
social_output_fraction            = 0
confirmed_output_fraction         = 0
communal_need_coverage[i]         = 0
communal_satisfaction_ratio       = undefined
```

The gap is immediate and damning. 77% of every dollar earned is already committed to survival before a single discretionary purchase. One missed paycheck triggers a food crisis. The dollar wage does not represent purchasing power so much as a thin margin above destitution. This number — $35 / month of genuine discretion — is the number the Observer will return to again and again.

## Phase 1: Committee Formation and the First Observer Report

### Scope A (Germany) — Months 1–4

The committee forms after a long-running overtime dispute. Seven delegates are elected, immediately revocable, bound by mandate. They exercise their **Access** right on day one: they request the full financial records, supplier contracts, customer agreements, and equipment registers. The managing director delays. The committee publishes the delay. Workers hold a meeting. The records appear the following week.

The Observer's first report emerges from six weeks of data collection:

```
Scope A — Observer Report, Month 4

Inputs:
  - Specialty cutting oils:      sourced from BASF (market)       → 9% of total input cost
  - Aluminium billet stock:      sourced from Hydro ASA (market)  → 61% of total input cost
  - Electricity:                 grid (market)                    → 14%
  - Tooling and inserts:         Sandvik, Kennametal (market)     → 16%

Outputs:
  - BMW suspension arms:         100% to BMW AG under 3-year contract
  - VW subframes:                100% to VW AG under 18-month contract
  - Scrap aluminium:             sold to recycler

Current Plan: fully market-facing. social_input_fraction = 0. social_output_fraction = 0.

Key findings:
  - Profit extracted to holding company last year: €2.3M (21% of revenue)
  - Average hourly wage: €21.20. Top management compensation: 9× median worker wage.
  - Maintenance backlog: 14 machines deferred. Capitalist's plan sacrifices long-term
    productive capacity for short-term margin extraction.
  - wage_rate_normalized for 4 senior technicians: 1.6 (elevated; not yet committee members)
```

The committee publishes this openly. Workers learn for the first time that €2.3 million left the factory last year while the roof leaked and two lathes sat idle. The exposure phase begins in earnest.

**Desired Plan (first draft):**

The committee identifies the cutting oils as **Autonomous** — a social-plan-affiliated industrial cooperative in the Ruhr already produces them sustainably. Switching requires no federation coordination; it is a supplier change. The committee proposes a trial.

The aluminium supply is **Social-Dependent**: no social plan source exists yet. The committee files a demand with the federation: _"We need a socially-organised aluminium smelter or recycler."_

---

### Scope B (Bangladesh) — Months 1–4

Committee formation is not a negotiation — it is a confrontation. Management fires three organizers in the first week. The committee goes underground, operating as a workers' health-and-safety group (legally protected) while building mandate. After a factory-wide work-to-rule lasting four days, all three are reinstated under federation pressure and international solidarity attention.

The first Observer report is compiled from time-cards, supply delivery records, and interviews with workers on each floor. The numbers are already known in their bones — now they are written down.

```
Scope B — Observer Report, Month 4

Inputs:
  - Fabric (polyester/cotton blend): sourced from Chinese market suppliers → 54% of input cost
  - Thread, buttons, zippers:        market                               → 18%
  - Electricity:                     state grid (subsidised)              → 11%
  - Packaging:                       market                               → 7%
  - Other:                                                                → 10%

Outputs:
  - 100% exported to H&M, Zara, Primark under rolling seasonal contracts.
    Contracts renegotiated quarterly; brands can exit with 6 weeks' notice.

Current Plan: fully market-facing. social_input_fraction = 0. social_output_fraction = 0.

Key findings:
  - Revenue per garment: $4.20. Worker wage per garment: $0.06.
  - Profit margin: 18% extracted to conglomerate holding company.
  - wage_rate_normalized for floor supervisors: 1.8 (elevated; not committee members,
    but a co-optation risk to watch)
  - effective_net_market_wage[i] = $35 / month. This is published at every floor meeting.
    Workers hold the number up: "This is what we actually have."
```

The committee publishes everything. The $0.06 per garment number appears in international press via solidarity networks. The brands experience reputational pressure — they will remember this when conflict arrives.

**Desired Plan (first draft):**

The committee identifies two **Autonomous** moves: (1) a workers' food cooperative from an affiliated rural scope can supply the factory canteen at direct cost, replacing market catering. (2) a local sewing cooperative can absorb informal mending and alterations work that currently goes to market vendors. These require no federation coordination.

The fabric supply is **Social-Dependent**: no social-plan cotton or polyester capacity exists. The federation is notified.

## Phase 2: First Steps up the Ladder — Rungs 1–3

### Scope A (Germany) — Months 5–14

**Rung 2 — Partial Trial (Inputs):**

The committee executes the cutting oil switch. The Ruhr cooperative delivers on time. Quality is equivalent; price is 4% lower. After three months: `social_input_fraction` moves from 0 to **0.07**. Workers notice nothing in their wage, but the committee publishes it: _"7% of our inputs now come from the social plan."_

**Rung 2 — Partial Trial (Outputs):**

A regional public transport authority operates 600 buses. Their maintenance workshop has been lobbying for reliable domestic suspension components — the market supply chain is long and expensive. The committee proposes: redirect 10% of suspension output to the transport authority, at agreed social prices, in exchange for the authority recording confirmed receipt EconomicEvents in the PlanStore.

BMW's contract allows up to 8% reduction in quarterly delivery — the committee stays just inside that limit. VW's contract is looser. After negotiation:

```
Month 14 — Scope A state:
  social_input_fraction       = 0.07   (cutting oils)
  social_output_fraction      = 0.10   (bus fleet maintenance components)
  confirmed_output_fraction   = 0.09   (transport authority confirms 90%; 10% quality dispute on tolerances)

  fairness_ratio = [social_real_wage_per_hour × 0.09] / [market_real_wage_per_hour × 0.90]
                = [€21.20 × 0.90 × 0.09] / [€21.20 × 1.00 × 0.90]
                ≈ 0.09 / 0.90 = 0.10   ← very low, expected at this stage
```

The substitution ratio is low because the market side still dominates. Workers are not losing money — they are still paid from market revenue. The ratio will move toward 1 only as `social_output_fraction` rises. The committee explains this clearly at the assembly. Workers accept it. What they notice more is the first communal intervention.

**Communal Coverage begins:**

The federation, responding to the committee's data, pilots a communal childcare scheme in three Dortmund neighbourhoods adjacent to the factory. Eighteen children of factory workers enrol. Cost to parents: zero.

```
communal_need_coverage[i] (workers with children in scheme) = €450 / month
effective_net_market_wage[i] (those workers)                = €2,050 + €450 = €2,500 equivalent purchasing power
communal_satisfaction_ratio                                 rising
```

Workers with children in the scheme are the most vocal advocates of moving faster up the ladder. They have already experienced the communal satisfaction argument in their own bodies. They do not need to be persuaded by theory.

---

### Scope B (Bangladesh) — Months 5–14

**Rung 2 — Partial Trial (Communal, not yet output):**

The food cooperative begins operating in Month 5. Meals that previously cost each worker $1.20/day (market canteen) now cost $0.00 — collectively provisioned from a federated rural scope 80km north that produces vegetables and rice.

```
communal_need_coverage[i]   = $45 / month  (food now fully covered)
uncovered_essential_expenses = $70 / month  (rent $35, schooling $20, healthcare $15)
effective_net_market_wage    = $150 - $70 = $80 / month   (up from $35)

communal_satisfaction_ratio = ([claim_capacity × claim_purchasing_power] + $45) / $80
```

This is the most important single intervention in the Bangladeshi transition. The workers' effective purchasing freedom has more than doubled — not through a wage increase but through communal provision. The minimum viable dollar wage has fallen from $115 to $70. Workers now have room that did not exist before.

**Rung 2 — Partial Trial (Outputs):**

A domestic school federation needs uniform fabric for 40,000 students. The committee negotiates: Horizon Garments will produce 5% of its output as school uniforms for social recipients (the school federation records confirmed receipt EconomicEvents). The brands notice no interruption — their orders remain fulfilled.

```
Month 14 — Scope B state:
  social_input_fraction       = 0       (fabric still market-sourced)
  social_output_fraction      = 0.05    (school uniforms)
  confirmed_output_fraction   = 0.05    (school federation confirms in full)
  communal_need_coverage[i]   = $45     (food)
  effective_net_market_wage   = $80

  fairness_ratio ≈ 0.05 / 0.95 ≈ small — expected; market side still dominates
  communal_satisfaction_ratio = rising; workers feel the difference already
```

The committee's assemblies now fill with workers who previously did not attend. The food cooperative is proof of concept. The school uniforms are the beginning of output transition.

## Phase 3: Escalation — Rungs 3–4

### Scope A (Germany) — Months 15–28

The committee evaluates the partial trial. The quality dispute (10% of social outputs unconfirmed) is resolved by sending a committee member to work alongside the transport authority's mechanics for two weeks — tolerance specifications are clarified, reworked, confirmed. `confirmed_output_fraction` rises to match `social_output_fraction`.

The committee proposes expanding to 30% social output. BMW's contract allows no further reduction. The committee now faces a fork: negotiate a revision to the BMW contract, or substitute BMW volume with social recipients.

They choose to negotiate first. BMW agrees — reluctantly — to a 15% volume reduction over six months, absorbing the shortfall from another supplier. The committee makes up the volume by supplying the public transport authority (now covering three cities) and a rail maintenance cooperative.

```
Month 28 — Scope A state:
  social_input_fraction       = 0.07    (cutting oils; aluminium social source still pending)
  social_output_fraction      = 0.30
  confirmed_output_fraction   = 0.29
  dollar_balance              = positive (federation provides Mechanism C bridge of €18k/month
                                          while market revenue falls)
  fairness_ratio              ≈ 0.85    (below parity but improving; workers accept it)
```

Meanwhile, communal provision deepens:

```
Month 28 — Communal Coverage (Scope A):
  communal childcare:         covers 70% of workers with children (€450/month each)
  communal housing pilot:     12 families in federation housing cooperative (€700/month saved)

  Average across all workers:
  communal_need_coverage[i]   ≈ €320 / month
  effective_net_market_wage   ≈ €2,050 - [market expenditure now reduced] ≈ net €1,730
  communal_satisfaction_ratio > 1 for workers in childcare and housing schemes
```

The committee's assembly votes 163–21 to push toward 50% social output. The minority — mostly older technicians with paid-off mortgages and no children — worry about their pension entitlements under a market contract regime. The committee opens a negotiation with the federation to cover those entitlements through the claims pool.

---

### Scope B (Bangladesh) — Months 15–28

The federation builds a workers' housing cooperative — a six-floor residential block near the factory, housing 200 workers. Rent: zero (collectively owned by the scope federation). Workers leaving private landlords or the company dormitory move in.

```
Month 20 — Communal Coverage Update (Scope B):
  food:                       $45 / month (fully covered, Month 5 onward)
  housing (200 workers):      $35 / month (covered for those in cooperative)
  communal_need_coverage[i]:
    workers in housing + food: $80 / month
    workers with food only:    $45 / month
  effective_net_market_wage:
    workers in housing + food: $150 - $35 = $115 ... wait:
    uncovered_essential_expenses (housing + food scheme): $35 (healthcare) + $20 (schooling)
                                                        = $55
    effective_net_market_wage: $150 - $55 = $95 / month
```

The workers in the housing and food scheme now have $95/month of real discretionary income — nearly three times their Month 0 figure of $35. The minimum viable dollar wage has fallen to $55 (the cost of what is not yet covered communally).

The committee proposes: raise `social_output_fraction` to 0.25. The reasoning at the assembly: _"We need $55/month to survive. Our wage is $150. We can afford to redirect 25% of production to social recipients even if our dollar wage falls 10%, because our survival threshold is now $55 not $115."_

```
Month 28 — Scope B state:
  social_output_fraction      = 0.25
  confirmed_output_fraction   = 0.24
  communal_need_coverage[avg] ≈ $62 / month (average across workers in and out of schemes)
  effective_net_market_wage   ≈ $88 / month
  fairness_ratio              ≈ 0.80   (gap between social and market side; expected)
  communal_satisfaction_ratio > 1 for workers in both schemes
```

The brands respond: Zara withdraws its seasonal contract. This removes 18% of the factory's dollar revenue overnight. The federation activates Mechanism C: `subsidy_received` = $22,000/month to bridge the dollar solvency gap. Workers vote not to reduce `social_output_fraction`. They vote to expand the food scheme to cover all 800 workers.

## Phase 4: Conflict — Rung 5

### Scope A (Germany) — Month 29

BMW terminates its contract. The reason given is "quality and delivery inconsistency"; the real reason — made plain by internal emails the committee has obtained via a sympathetic whistleblower — is that BMW's supplier management team has flagged the scope as a "transitional risk." Legal action threatens over alleged contract breach.

The committee's response:

1. Publish the internal BMW emails immediately — full transparency.
2. Call an emergency assembly. Workers vote 178–9 to occupy the factory floor and continue production for social recipients at full capacity.
3. Notify the federation: "We need legal support, full dollar bridge, and absorption of all output."
4. Social plan defense units take posts at the factory gates. Scab recruitment attempts by the holding company are publicly named.

The holding company seeks a court injunction. The court grants a temporary restraining order. Workers remain in the factory. Regional solidarity — metalworkers from three other Ruhr plants, students, and public transit workers — surround the building. The transit workers, whose buses now depend on Rheinmetall Precision suspension parts, join the picket.

```
Month 31 — CONFLICT state (Scope A):
  social_output_fraction      = 0.30 → workers hold, refuse to reduce
  confirmed_output_fraction   = 0.30 (transit authority accelerates confirmations)
  subsidy_received            = €45,000 / month (federation emergency support)
  dollar_balance              = borderline solvent
  fairness_ratio              = 0.78 (falling — market side collapsing; social side
                                      must compensate)
  communal_satisfaction_ratio = 1.2 (rising fast as childcare + housing coverage grows
                                      even during conflict — community mobilises)
```

The workers in the childcare scheme — who would have been most exposed to a wage cut — are among the most resolute. Their effective_net_market_wage was already absorbing the change through communal coverage. The conflict lasts eight weeks. The holding company, facing a months-long legal battle against a federation with resources and a public mobilisation, accepts a settlement: the scope transitions to workers' cooperative management. BMW is replaced by public transit and rail infrastructure contracts arranged by the federation.

---

### Scope B (Bangladesh) — Month 22

H&M cancels its order three months earlier than expected — the reputational exposure from the Observer's published data (the $0.06 per garment figure) has made the contract politically expensive. They publicly announce "ethical sourcing concerns" while terminating, a cynical inversion that enrages the workers.

This removes 35% of dollar revenue. The committee has seen this coming. At the Month 20 assembly they voted to prepare for it. The food cooperative, the housing block, and the school uniform contracts are the preparation.

```
Month 22 — CONFLICT state (Scope B):
  social_output_fraction      = 0.35   (raised proactively the week H&M cancelled)
  confirmed_output_fraction   = 0.33
  subsidy_received            = $38,000 / month (federation emergency bridge)
  dollar_balance              = tight but solvent
  effective_net_market_wage   ≈ $88 (has not fallen significantly because communal
                                      coverage has been rising)
  communal_satisfaction_ratio = 1.6   (claims pool + communal provision now exceeds
                                       the reduced effective market wage)
```

The local police attempt to arrest three committee members on "economic sabotage" charges — a law used against labour organisers, never enforced against capitalists who abruptly terminate contracts. An international solidarity campaign forces the charges dropped within four days.

The state garment board, which has ties to the owning conglomerate, attempts to revoke the factory's export license. The committee responds: _"We are no longer primarily an export facility. Our primary customer is the social plan."_ The social recipients — school federations, a hospital system, three rural cooperatives needing work clothing — file demand records in the PlanStore.

---

## Phase 5: Consolidation and Full Social — Rungs 7–8

### Scope A (Germany) — Months 32–48

The settlement establishes a workers' cooperative. The holding company sells its equity stake at book value to the federation. The scope is now owned by its workers through the federation.

The committee becomes the managing body. The PlanStore transitions from "Desired Plan" to "Current Plan." The workers' Desired Plan is now the operative one.

```
Month 48 — Consolidated State (Scope A):
  social_input_fraction       = 0.35   (cutting oils, some tooling from social sources;
                                         federation aluminium smelter cooperation under way)
  social_output_fraction      = 0.75
  confirmed_output_fraction   = 0.74
  fairness_ratio              = 0.97   (near parity; claims pool has deepened)
  communal_need_coverage[avg] = €720 / month
      childcare:              €450 × 65% of workers = covered
      housing cooperative:    €700 × 30% of workers = covered
      average:                €720
  effective_net_market_wage   ≈ €1,330 / month
  communal_satisfaction_ratio = 1.4
  wage_rate_normalized        = 1.00 for all workers (differentials dissolved in cooperative)
```

The maintenance backlog — the 14 deferred machines that sat idle under capitalist ownership — has been cleared. The Desired Plan honored replenishment demands; the Current Plan never did.

The remaining 25% market output serves specialized customers (high-tolerance aerospace components) for whom no social plan recipient exists yet. The committee treats this as a shrinking residual. Within three years they expect `social_output_fraction` to reach 0.90.

---

### Scope B (Bangladesh) — Months 24–42

The conglomerate owner, facing a factory that no longer serves the brands whose relationships gave him political cover, attempts to sell the facility. No buyer appears — the committee has publicly documented every structural fault, deferred maintenance issue, and fire-safety violation in the building. The asking price drops. The owner eventually abandons formal management; the committee fills the vacuum.

The scope is brought under federation management. The PlanStore's Desired Plan becomes the operating plan.

```
Month 42 — Consolidated State (Scope B):
  social_input_fraction       = 0.10   (some organic cotton from federated rural scope;
                                         fabric supply still mostly market — Social-Dependent
                                         plan for domestic textile federation is active)
  social_output_fraction      = 0.70
  confirmed_output_fraction   = 0.69
  fairness_ratio              = 0.90   (approaching parity)

  communal_need_coverage[avg] = $98 / month
      food:                   $45   (all 800 workers)
      housing:                $35   (520 workers now in cooperative housing)
      federated schooling:    $18   (children of workers in federation schools, free)
      average across workers: $98

  effective_net_market_wage   ≈ $52 / month
      uncovered: healthcare ($15) + remaining personal expenses

  communal_satisfaction_ratio = 2.1   (total social value far exceeds market wage remainder)
  wage_rate_normalized        = 1.00 (supervisor differentials dissolved)
```

The factory now produces for: the domestic school federation (uniforms), a hospital consortium (medical garments and protective clothing), regional cooperatives (work clothing, protective gear), and an agricultural federation (durable field clothing). 30% of output still goes to an ethical market buyer — a European workers' cooperative retailer — providing dollar revenue that sustains `subsidy_received` needs while the federation builds domestic fabric capacity.

The workers' food cooperative has expanded to supply three surrounding neighbourhoods. The housing block has a waiting list. The committee is now training workers from two nearby factories — not yet in transition — in Observer procedure.

## Comparing the Two Trajectories

| Metric                                                      | Scope A (Germany)                       | Scope B (Bangladesh)                    |
| :---------------------------------------------------------- | :-------------------------------------- | :-------------------------------------- |
| Starting `effective_net_market_wage` as % of `dollar_wage`  | 59%                                     | 23%                                     |
| Month communal coverage first exceeded $50/month equivalent | Month 22                                | Month 5                                 |
| Month CONFLICT reached                                      | Month 29                                | Month 22                                |
| `communal_satisfaction_ratio` at time of CONFLICT           | 1.2                                     | 1.6                                     |
| Primary driver of worker resolve during CONFLICT            | Childcare + housing scheme participants | Food + housing scheme participants      |
| Speed of positive feedback loop                             | Moderate                                | Fast                                    |
| Federation dollar bridge peak                               | €45k/month (8 weeks)                    | $38k/month (ongoing)                    |
| `social_output_fraction` at consolidation                   | 0.75                                    | 0.70                                    |
| Residual market dependency at consolidation                 | Specialty aerospace contracts           | Ethical export buyer for dollar revenue |
| `wage_rate_normalized` convergence                          | Month 38                                | Month 30                                |

**Why does the feedback loop fire faster in Bangladesh?**

At Month 0, $115 of every $150 dollar wage goes to survival. A $45 communal food provision is not a supplement — it is a 30% reduction in survival costs. The minimum viable dollar wage falls from $115 to $70 in a single move. The political consequence is immediate: workers who previously could not afford to risk their wage can now risk a portion of it. Each additional unit of communal provision has outsized political effect because it frees income that was entirely bound.

In Germany, the math is less acute. €450 of communal childcare against €2,050 of effective net income is significant — but workers are not one missed payment from catastrophe. The feedback loop runs, but more slowly. The political resolve during CONFLICT comes later and depends more on solidarity networks (the transit workers whose buses depend on Rheinmetall parts) than on the immediate metabolic relief that drives Bangladeshi workers.

**The claims pool and its character:**

In Germany at consolidation, the claims pool is used for: tools, holiday travel, cultural events, sports equipment, electronic goods. Survival is covered communally. The assembly debates whether to expand the claims pool or deepen communal provision — a genuinely political question about individual vs collective appropriation.

In Bangladesh at consolidation, the claims pool is still partly survival-oriented (healthcare is not yet fully communalised). But its character is shifting. Workers who a year ago were spending 77% of income on survival now spend 37%. The assembly debates a communal healthcare fund — one that would reduce `uncovered_essential_expenses` by a further $15/month and raise `effective_net_market_wage` to near-full discretionary status.

## What the Observer Sees That the Capitalist Cannot

In both scopes, the Observer's published data performs a function beyond accounting. It makes visible what market prices mystify.

In Bangladesh, the $0.06 per garment figure says: the worker who sews this shirt receives six cents from its $4.20 social product. The gap is not efficiency — it is power. The brands' profit is not a reward for productive contribution; it is the residue of the power to set prices and the power to fire.

In Germany, the €2.3 million extracted by the holding company says: 200 workers produced this value; zero of the €2.3 million went to maintaining the 14 deferred machines; all of it went to the holding company's portfolio. Productive capacity was sacrificed for liquidity.

Neither scope could have known these numbers before the committee used its Access right. The first act of the Scope Committee — demanding the books — is already transition. The numbers are not neutral. They are organizing tools.

```
Observer finding that changes everything:

  Scope A: "The capitalist extracted €2.3M while 14 machines sat unrepaired."
  Scope B: "Your effective_net_market_wage is $35. Everything else is already owed to survival."
```

These are the numbers that get posted on the break room wall. They are the numbers that make the next assembly attendance double. They are the numbers that make the vote to increase `social_output_fraction` something other than abstract.

## The Communal Satisfaction Feedback Loop: a Diagram in Words

The transition is not a single line from 0 to 1 on a single axis. It is two interlocked processes that reinforce each other:

```
Process 1 — The Substitution Ladder:
  social_output_fraction increases
  → market_revenue falls
  → dollar_wage falls (or subsidy_received compensates)
  → fairness_ratio must be maintained
  → federation deepens the claims pool

Process 2 — The Communal Satisfaction Loop:
  social plan expands communal provision
  → communal_need_coverage[i] rises
  → uncovered_essential_expenses[i] falls
  → effective_net_market_wage[i] shrinks as a SURVIVAL requirement
  → minimum viable dollar_wage falls
  → workers can accept lower dollar_wage without real hardship
  → resistance to social_output_fraction increases falls
  → Process 1 can advance faster
  → social plan has more capacity → communal provision can expand further
  → loop repeats
```

The two processes are not independent. The communal satisfaction loop is what makes Process 1 politically sustainable. Without it, every increase in `social_output_fraction` is a wage cut in disguise, and workers will eventually refuse. With it, every increase in communal coverage lowers the effective cost of the wage cut, until the question _"can we afford to transition?"_ becomes _"what is there left to lose?"_

## What Can Go Wrong

**Co-optation:** `wage_rate_normalized` for committee members diverging upward. In Scope A, two committee members accepted consultancy fees from the holding company during the negotiation phase. The assembly caught it because the Observer reports per-worker compensation. Both were immediately recalled. This is what imperative mandate and immediate revocability are for.

**Confirmation lag:** In Scope B, the school federation delayed confirming receipt of 4,000 uniforms for six weeks due to internal bureaucratic disputes. `confirmed_output_fraction` lagged `social_output_fraction` by 0.04. Workers' `validated_hours` and thus `claim_capacity` fell below expectation. The committee published the lag openly, named the unconfirmed quantity, and pressured the school federation to resolve the dispute. It did. This is what transparent Observer reporting is for.

**Dollar bridge dependency:** In Scope B, `subsidy_received` from the federation ran for fourteen months. The federation attached a condition: Mechanism A (domestic fabric cooperative) must show operational progress within twelve months or the bridge phases out. This prevented `subsidy_received` from becoming a permanent substitute for building social input capacity. It is not yet resolved — the domestic fabric cooperative is at Rung 2.

**Infeasible plans:** In Scope A, the committee proposed converting 20% of machining capacity to produce wind turbine components. The engineering analysis (conducted by a technical consultant) found: the existing CNC machines cannot hold the tolerances required without a €4M retooling investment the federation cannot currently fund. The plan is classified **Infeasible** — not abandoned but deferred. It remains on the Desired Plan as a horizon that may become reachable as the federation's capacity grows.

**Repression:** In Scope B, the state. The economic sabotage charges were dropped. The export license threat failed. But 23 workers were detained overnight in Month 23. Two had injuries consistent with mistreatment. The social plan defense units that secured the factory gates did not prevent state action against individual workers away from the factory. This gap — between what the committee can defend at the point of production and what the state can do in the street, in the courthouse, in the worker's home — is not closed by the Observer's report or the claims pool. It requires organisation beyond the scope: solidarity networks, legal defence funds, political power at the level where police are commanded.

## Summary: What the Framework Gives You

The transition framework from spec.md is not a theory of how workers should feel. It is a measurement apparatus. It converts the class struggle from a moral argument into a visible set of numbers — numbers that workers can check, dispute, and act on.

| What the framework measures                             | What it reveals                                                             |
| :------------------------------------------------------ | :-------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| `social_output_fraction` vs `confirmed_output_fraction` | Whether social deliveries are real or only claimed                          |
| `fairness_ratio`                                        | Whether the transition is paying workers equitably as market revenue falls  |
| `effective_net_market_wage`                             | What the dollar wage is actually worth after survival costs                 |
| `communal_satisfaction_ratio`                           | Whether the total social plan value exceeds what the market wage must cover |
| `wage_rate_normalized`                                  | Whether the committee is separating from those it represents                |
| `dollar_balance`                                        | Whether the scope is metabolically solvent through the transition           |

The numbers do not make the decision. Workers make the decision — at assemblies, under binding mandate, with immediate revocability of anyone who departs from the mandate. The numbers make the decision legible. They make it possible to say: _"Here is exactly how much we can move. Here is exactly what it costs. Here is exactly who benefits and who is exposed. Now let us vote."_

That is what the Observer is for. That is what the claims pool is for. That is what the committee is for. The social plan is not a command from above — it is the emergent form of workers who have decided to see the whole, and to act on what they see.
