## `Scope Committees`

_`Scopes are spatial assemblages of economic resources â€” factories, farms, mines, transport networks, research institutions. Some resources within a scope are untransportable without disassembly. The question before us: what recipes are possible using these untransportable economic resources? What can they be repurposed for? What can be made by disassembling them?`_

**`Scope Committee Formation:`**  
_`Workers in each scope elect from among themselves a Scope Committee, with delegates immediately revocable and bound by imperative mandate. The committee stewards the organization of the production process within that scope.`_

- **`Rights:`**
  - **`Decisions:`** `for all scope-participants on matters internal to the scope's production process, subject to federation decisions on matters of broader metabolic interdependence`
  - **`Immediately-Revoke:`**
    - **`Scope-Participants`** `(any worker may be removed from the committee by their electors)`
    - **`Delegates`** `to parent-scope assemblies`
    - **`Technical Consultants`** `(specialists called in as advisors, not decision-makers)`
  - **`Access:`** `Full visibility into all scope operations, including:`
    - `Business secrets, financial records, supply contracts, customer lists`
    - `Technical specifications and recipes`
    - `Maintenance schedules and equipment condition`
    - `Labor processes and skill inventories`
    - `Input sources and output destinations`
  - **`Communicate:`** `Publish all doings and shortcomings â€” to workers within the scope, to the parent federation, to the public`
  - **`Inspect:`** `Send observers to suppliers, customers, and related scopes to trace the metabolic connections`
  - **`Propose:`** `Alternative production plans, repurposing of equipment, disassembly recipes`
  - **`Withdraw:`** `The right to initiate withdrawal from a federation, subject to settlement of obligations (see: Settlement of Obligations Upon Withdrawal)`

- **`Responsibilities:`**
  - **`To electors (scope-participants):`**
    - `Regular reporting and open meetings`
    - `Publication of all records obtained through access rights`
    - `Faithful execution of binding instructions`
    - `Training and mentorship in economic literacy â€” turning all workers into planners`
  - **`To parent federation:`**
    - `Provide accurate data on scope capacity, input requirements, and output potential`
    - `Abide by federation decisions on matters delegated upward (inter-scope coordination, transport, etc.)`
    - `Where federation decision conflicts with binding instructions on a matter outside federation authority, seek planning-based dispute resolution`
  - **`To external-constituency:`**
    - `Consider interests of those affected by scope operations (downstream communities, neighbors, future inhabitants)`
    - `Grant standing to affected interests in deliberations that materially concern them`
  - **`To the movement as a whole:`**
    - `Prepare for direct workers' management when conditions permit`
    - `Document all learning â€” create recipes for how other scopes can do the same`

**`Scope Committee Tasks:`**

| `Phase`                  | `Activity`                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                              |
| :----------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| `Infiltration & Mapping` | `Workers use their access rights to document everything. Not passive observation â€” active investigation. What actually happens here? Where do inputs come from? Where do outputs go? Who decides? What are the metabolic flows? What skills exist? What's hidden? Build the real PlanStore from below.` |
| `Exposure`               | `Publish business secrets. Make visible to all workers â€” and to society â€” the actual accounts: what capitalists extract, what gets wasted, what's squandered through anarchy and profit-seeking. Show the debits and credits of society starting from this scope.`                                      |
| `Control`                | `Begin intervening. Not yet management, but oversight. Challenge decisions that harm workers or communities. Demand justification. Use the metabolic visibility to show consequences. Build worker confidence and competence.`                                                                          |
| `Planning`               | `Develop alternative plans from within the scope. Show what could be produced, how work could be organized, what would change if workers managed. Use the same planning tools â€” PlanNetters, indexes, recipes â€” but now with real data from inside.`                                                    |
| `Management`             | `When conditions permit â€” when the old power is broken or withdrawn â€” transition from control to direct management. The committee becomes the managing body. The PlanStore becomes binding. The scope operates as a cooperative within the federation.`                                                 |

**`Scope Committee Composition:`**

- **`Elected Delegates:`** `Workers from within the scope, chosen by their peers, immediately revocable, bound by imperative mandate`
- **`Technical Consultants:`** `Accountants, statisticians, engineers, scientists, and others called in as needed â€” as advisors, not decision-makers. Selected for sincere devotion to the people, not for technocratic authority.`
- **`Affected Interest Representatives:`** `Where scope operations materially affect non-workers (neighbors, downstream communities), those affected have standing to send observers or, in some cases, delegates with limited participation rights defined by the relevant federation.`

**`Scope Hierarchy:`**  
_`Scopes nest according to metabolic interdependence:`_

- **`Workplace Scope:`** `Single factory, farm, mine, office, laboratory`
- **`Enterprise Scope:`** `Multiple workplaces under common ownership or coordination (a trust, a supply chain, a company town)`
- **`Branch Scope:`** `Whole industries â€” auto, steel, textiles, software, transport`
- **`Regional Scope:`** `All scopes within a bioregion or administrative area`
- **`National Scope:`** `All scopes within a nation`
- **`International Scope:`** `Global coordination of branches (rare, for genuinely global industries)`

_`At each level, scope committees elect delegates to the next level up, with binding mandates and immediate revocability. The higher-level committee coordinates what cannot be coordinated below: inter-scope transport, shared infrastructure, industry-wide standards, balancing of surpluses and deficits across the branch.`_

**`Dual Power Within the Scope:`**

_`At first, the Scope Committee changes nothing about ownership or legal authority. The capitalist still owns; the manager still manages; the shareholder still extracts. But the condition of possibility for fundamental change is set.`_

- `The capitalist's power is no longer absolute. Every decision can be scrutinized. Every secret can be exposed. Every lie can be countered with visible metabolic truth.`
- `The workers' power grows through exercise. Each successful intervention builds confidence. Each mapped process builds competence. Each exposed secret builds solidarity.`
- `When the moment arrives â€” crisis, strike, revolutionary transition, capitalist flight â€” the committee is ready. It has the data, the plans, the skills, the legitimacy. It steps into the vacuum.`

_`Dual power is not a stable equilibrium. It is a tension that must resolve either in the recommodification of the committee (its incorporation into management, its separation from workers) or in the expropriation of the capitalist and the assumption of direct workers' management.`_

**`On Public Works and Unemployment:`**

_`The struggle against unemployment cannot be separated from the struggle for planned economy. Public works are not mere relief â€” they are opportunities to build metabolic capacity while meeting human needs.`_

- **`Demand:`** `Public works shall be organized on a scale commensurate with unemployment, and shall be integrated into a general plan covering a considerable number of years`
- **`Transition:`** `Where private businesses close due to crisis, workers shall demand they resume operation as public utilities under direct workers' management`
- **`Control becomes Management:`** `In such cases, workers' control is insufficient â€” only direct management can reorganize production for public need rather than private profit`
- **`Planning Horizon:`** `The plan for public works shall look forward decades, not months, and shall be developed through the same committee structure, with full worker and community participation`

**`On the Relation to Capitalist Authority:`**

_`Scope Committees do not ask permission. They assert the right of workers to know, to scrutinize, to expose, to propose. This right is not granted by law â€” it is taken by organization.`_

- `The capitalist who opens their books voluntarily usually does so to justify wage cuts. The workers' answer: we are not interested in the bookkeeping of individual bankrupts, but in the account ledgers of all exploiters as a whole. We will not accommodate our living standards to your crisis. We will reorganize the whole system.`
- `The capitalist who refuses access confirms what workers already suspected: there is something to hide. The committee publishes that refusal. The pressure mounts.`
- `The state that protects business secrets reveals itself as the committee of the bourgeoisie. The workers' answer: dual power. Our committee, not your court, will decide what must be visible.`

**`On the School of Planning:`**

_`Workers' control is a school for planned economy. Not a classroom â€” a battlefield. Workers learn planning not from textbooks but from the struggle to make production visible, to trace metabolic connections, to expose capitalist waste, to propose alternatives.`_

- **`First lesson:`** `The books are not sacred. They can be opened, read, understood.`
- **`Second lesson:`** `The numbers tell a story. Follow the inputs, follow the outputs, follow the money. See who benefits, who pays, who decides.`
- **`Third lesson:`** `Alternatives are possible. Different inputs, different processes, different products, different distributions. The same tools that capitalists use to exploit can be used to emancipate.`
- **`Fourth lesson:`** `Planning is power. Those who can see the whole can coordinate the parts. Those who can coordinate can transform.`

_`When the hour strikes for direct management, the proletariat will be ready â€” not because they studied, but because they struggled.`_

## `Gradual Transition Within the Scope`

_`The scope exists within capitalism. Its current operation depends on inputs bought on markets and outputs sold on markets. It cannot simply sever these connections overnight without ceasing to produce. The transition from capitalist operation to associated production must be gradual, deliberate, and metabolically safe.`_

**`Two Plans, One Scope:`**

_`Each Scope Committee maintains two parallel plans in its PlanStore:`_

| `Plan`         | `Description`                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                          |
| :------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| `Current Plan` | `The plan that actually governs production today. Inputs sourced from markets. Outputs sold to markets. Labor organized according to capitalist command. This plan is largely determined by private interests â€” the capitalist's profit calculations, supply contracts, customer relationships, legal obligations. The Scope Committee maps it, exposes it, but does not yet control it.`                                              |
| `Desired Plan` | `The plan the workers would implement if they managed. Inputs chosen for metabolic sustainability, not lowest cost. Outputs chosen for social need, not profitable demand. Labor organized cooperatively, not hierarchically. Replenishment demands honored. Reserves maintained. This plan is determined by the Scope Committee through democratic deliberation, binding mandates, and the full participation of scope-participants.` |

_`The gap between Current Plan and Desired Plan is the measure of capitalist distortion. Closing that gap is the work of transition.`_

**`Gradual Substitution:`**

_`The scope cannot jump from Current Plan to Desired Plan in one bound. Its current inputs come from specific suppliers, often capitalist firms that would cut it off if it stopped paying. Its current outputs go to specific customers, often capitalist firms that would switch suppliers if it stopped delivering. To attempt immediate substitution is to invite metabolic collapse â€” the factory stops, workers are laid off, the committee loses credibility.`_

_`Instead, the scope pursues gradual substitution:`_

- **`Inputs:`** `Over successive planning cycles, replace a portion of market-sourced inputs with socially-sourced inputs. Each replacement must be metabolically viable â€” the new input must arrive on time, in sufficient quantity, at acceptable quality. The social plan (the federation of scopes) becomes a partial supplier alongside the market.`
- **`Outputs:`** `Over successive planning cycles, redirect a portion of output from market customers to social recipients. Each redirection must be metabolically viable â€” the social recipient must actually need and use the output, and must provide metabolic return (replenishment, labor, etc.) that sustains the scope. The social plan becomes a partial customer alongside the market.`

_`The proportion shifts gradually: `social_input_fraction`and`social_output_fraction` each move from ~0 toward 1 over successive planning cycles â€” 10%, 25%, 50%, 75%, 90%, 100%. At each step, the scope verifies that the new metabolic connections actually work â€” that inputs arrive, that outputs are used and confirmed by recipients, that replenishment flows, that the scope remains dollar-solvent.`_

**`Three Classes of Plans:`**

_`The Scope Committee evaluates possible plans along two axes: whether they are feasible without social plan support, and whether they are feasible with social plan support. This yields three classes:`_

| `Class`            | `Feasible Without Social Plan?` | `Feasible With Social Plan?` | `Transition Path`                                                                                                                                          |
| :----------------- | :------------------------------ | :--------------------------- | :--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| `Autonomous`       | `Yes`                           | `Yes`                        | `Can be implemented immediately, regardless of federation. These are the first steps â€” low-hanging fruit that requires no coordination.`                   |
| `Social-Dependent` | `No`                            | `Yes`                        | `Cannot be implemented until the federation provides the necessary inputs or absorbs the necessary outputs. These become demands on the social plan.`      |
| `Infeasible`       | `No`                            | `No`                         | `Cannot be implemented at all with current technology, resources, or metabolic constraints. These must be abandoned or postponed until conditions change.` |

_`The Scope Committee prioritizes Autonomous plans first â€” they build momentum, demonstrate the committee's capacity, and create material improvements that deepen worker commitment. It then brings Social-Dependent plans to the federation, arguing for the support needed to make them viable. Infeasible plans are set aside, but not forgotten â€” they may become feasible as technology advances or as the federation's capacity grows.`_

**`Social Plan as Enabler:`**

_`The federation of scopes (the social plan) exists to enable transitions that individual scopes cannot accomplish alone. Its role is not to command, but to supply and absorb â€” to provide the inputs that Autonomous plans cannot source, to accept the outputs that Autonomous plans cannot sell.`_

- **`Supply-Side Support:`** `When a scope's Desired Plan requires inputs not available through markets (sustainably grown cotton, non-toxic dyes, repairable components), the federation searches for scopes that can produce them. If none exist, it may seed new production â€” a farm converted to organic methods, a chemical plant retooled for safe inputs, a manufacturing cooperative formed to make the components.`
- **`Demand-Side Support:`** `When a scope's Desired Plan produces outputs that markets won't buy (durable goods, repairable designs, socially useful but unprofitable products), the federation becomes a customer. It distributes the outputs to other scopes that need them, or to social consumption (schools, hospitals, public works), or to reserves. It provides metabolic return â€” labor, replenishment, resources â€” that keeps the producing scope viable.`

_`The social plan is not a centralized planner. It is the emergent coordination of scopes that have chosen to cooperate. Its capacity grows as more scopes join, as more Autonomous plans succeed, as more Social-Dependent plans are attempted and validated.`_

**`Communal Satisfaction of Need:`**

_`The social plan satisfies needs not only through individual claim but also through communal provision â€” housing, healthcare, childcare, food security, public transport. Where these are collectively organised, workers no longer purchase them on the market individually.`_

_`Communal satisfaction of need is distinct from market satisfaction of need not by the goods themselves but by the social relation: market satisfaction binds need to dollar income, creating dependence on wage-setting and employment. Communal satisfaction dissolves that binding â€” need is met through collective production and validated by use, not by price.`_

_`This creates a positive feedback loop:`_

1. _`As communal provision expands, `communal_need_coverage[i]`rises and`uncovered_essential_expenses[i]` falls.`_
2. _`The minimum viable dollar wage falls â€” workers can tolerate a lower `dollar_wage[i]` because survival needs are met communally, not through market income.`_
3. _`Lower minimum viable dollar wage reduces resistance to increasing `social_output_fraction`, which reduces `market_revenue` and thus dollar wages.`_
4. _`Faster transition deepens the social plan's productive capacity, enabling further communal provision â€” closing the loop.`_

_`This feedback runs independently of the substitution ratio. A scope can hold a balanced substitution ratio while its communal satisfaction ratio rises steadily, signaling that the transition is deepening even where individual hourly compensation appears stable.`_

_`The effect is strongest in low-income regions, where a larger fraction of income covers bare essentials. Communal provision of housing, healthcare, and childcare alone can transform the political economy of transition without touching the individual claims pool â€” whose character shifts from a survival instrument to a discretionary one as communal coverage grows.`_

**`Input Substitution Mechanisms:`**

_`Increasing `social_input_fraction` â€” the fraction of inputs sourced from the social plan â€” is not a single action. There are three distinct mechanisms, each with different preconditions, timelines, and dollar-flow consequences. They are not mutually exclusive; a scope may use all three simultaneously for different inputs.`_

**`Mechanism A â€” New Social Provider:`**

_`The federation creates or seeds a new socially-organised source for this input. Examples: converting a conventional farm to organic methods to supply a textile mill; forming a manufacturing cooperative to produce repairable components; retooling a chemical plant for non-toxic dyes.`_

- `social_input_fraction` increases as the new provider comes online.
- Slowest to establish, but most durable: the new capacity is wholly within the social plan from the start.
- Represented in the PlanStore as a new Plan for the provider scope, with its own `social_output_fraction` trajectory. The two scopes' transitions are linked: the new provider's social output is this scope's social input.
- Classified as **Social-Dependent** until the new provider is operational; Autonomous once it is.

**`Mechanism B â€” Supplier Partial Transition:`**

_`An existing supplier â€” still capitalist-owned â€” has its own Scope Committee. That committee negotiates with the receiving scope's committee to redirect a fraction of their output to the social plan rather than the market.`_

- `social_input_fraction` increases as the supplier's `social_output_fraction` increases.
- The two scopes' transitions are coupled: each scope's `social_output_fraction` and `social_input_fraction` move together. A supplier that stalls pulls back the receiving scope's input transition.
- Faster to begin than Mechanism A (no new capacity needed), but less stable: the supplier remains partially capitalist and the arrangement can be withdrawn.
- The federation planner must hold both scopes' transition trajectories in the same PlanStore to detect coupling and route compensation.

**`Mechanism C â€” Dollar Bridge:`**

_`The federation provides `subsidy_received`so the scope can continue purchasing inputs from market suppliers while`social_output_fraction` increases (reducing market revenue) and while Mechanisms A or B are being established.`_

- Does **not** increase `social_input_fraction`. Inputs still come from the market.
- Compensates for the dollar-solvency gap: as `social_output_fraction` rises, `market_revenue` falls, but `market_input_cost` remains (until A or B mature). `subsidy_received` bridges this gap.
- A **bridge only**: dollar subsidies without concurrently building Mechanism A or B create dependency, not transition. The federation should attach a timeline â€” Mechanism C phases out as A or B come online.
- Represented in the PlanStore as a federation transfer to the scope: visible in `dollar_balance` but not in physical flow fractions.

**`Summary:`**

| Mechanism                       | Increases `social_input_fraction`? | Timeline  | Stability                                     | Dollar impact                                                  |
| :------------------------------ | :--------------------------------- | :-------- | :-------------------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------- |
| A â€” New social provider         | Yes                                | Slow      | High â€” dedicated social capacity              | Reduces `market_input_cost` once online                        |
| B â€” Supplier partial transition | Yes                                | Medium    | Medium â€” depends on supplier's own transition | Reduces `market_input_cost` proportionally                     |
| C â€” Dollar bridge               | No                                 | Immediate | Low â€” dependency risk if permanent            | Adds `subsidy_received` to cover unchanged `market_input_cost` |

**`Output Substitution:`**

_`Output substitution â€” increasing `social_output_fraction` â€” has a single primary mechanism: redirecting a fraction of production from market customers to named social recipients. The social recipient replaces the market customer for that fraction of output. What varies is which social recipient absorbs the output:`_

- **`Direct social consumption:`** A commune, school, hospital, or public works body receives the output for end use.
- **`Intermediate social supply:`** Another transitioning scope receives the output as an input â€” their `social_input_fraction` increases via this scope's `social_output_fraction`.
- **`Reserve or buffer:`** A federation-held buffer absorbs the output. Especially useful when social demand is not yet organised but the scope wants to begin the transition.

_`Unlike input substitution, output substitution does not require a new producer â€” only a named social recipient who records a receive EconomicEvent. The constraint is demand-side: are there social recipients who need and will confirm receipt of this output? This is what Demand-Side Support (above) exists to organise.`_

**`The Transition Ladder:`**

_`For each input or output targeted for substitution, the scope climbs a ladder of increasing social integration:`_

| `Rung`                     | `Relationship to Market`            | `Relationship to Social Plan`                                                    |
| :------------------------- | :---------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| `1. Observation`           | `Market supplies/absorbs 100%`      | `Social plan maps flows, identifies opportunities`                               |
| `2. Partial Trial`         | `Market supplies/absorbs 90%`       | `Social plan supplies/absorbs 10% on trial basis`                                |
| `3. Evaluation`            | `Both relationships continue`       | `Scope evaluates: Did social supply work? Quality? Reliability? Metabolic cost?` |
| `4. Expansion`             | `Market share reduced to 75%`       | `Social share increased to 25%`                                                  |
| `5. Iteration`             | `Repeat steps 3-4 at 50%, 75%, 90%` | `Social plan capacity grows with demand`                                         |
| `6. Full Social Provision` | `Market share 0%`                   | `Social plan supplies/absorbs 100%`                                              |

_`At any rung, if the social plan fails to deliver, the scope can fall back to the market. This safety net is essential â€” it allows ambitious transition without catastrophic risk. The market remains as a backup, a teacher, and a reminder of what is being overcome.`_

**`Conflict Between Current and Desired:`**

_`The scope operates two plans, but it has only one set of machines, one workforce, one flow of inputs and outputs. When Current Plan and Desired Plan conflict â€” when they compete for the same resources, the same labor, the same capacity â€” how is the conflict resolved?`_

- **`During Control Phase:`** `The capitalist's plan prevails. The committee observes, exposes, challenges, but does not yet manage. Conflict is pedagogical â€” it reveals the gap between what is and what could be.`
- **`During Transition Phase:`** `The scope begins to implement Autonomous plans alongside the capitalist's plan. This creates dualism within the scope: part of production serves capital, part serves the associated producers. Conflict is managed through negotiation, through worker pressure, through the growing strength of the committee.`
- **`During Management Phase:`** `The capitalist is gone. The scope implements its Desired Plan across all operations. Conflict is internal â€” between different worker preferences, different technical possibilities, different ecological constraints â€” and is resolved through democratic process.`

_`The transition from Control to Management is not a single event. It is a process of gradually shifting the balance, gradually replacing capitalist command with associated production, gradually rendering the old power irrelevant.`_

**`Example: The Factory Floor`**

_`A textile mill. Current Plan: buy cheap synthetic fiber from petrochemical suppliers, weave into fabric, sell to fast-fashion brands. Desired Plan: buy organic cotton from regional farms, weave into durable fabric, sell to clothing cooperatives and public works.`_

- **`Rung 1 (Observation):`** `Committee maps the fiber supply chain. Discovers the petrochemical sources, the transport routes, the environmental damage. Publishes the findings.`
- **`Rung 2 (Trial):`** `Committee negotiates with a regional organic farm. The farm agrees to supply 5% of the mill's fiber needs, at a price slightly above market. The mill's capitalist owner objects, but the committee argues it's a "market test" of alternative materials. The trial proceeds.`
- **`Rung 3 (Evaluation):`** `The organic fiber works. Machines need minor adjustment, but fabric quality is good. Workers prefer handling it (no chemical smell). The committee documents everything.`
- **`Rung 4 (Expansion):`** `Committee proposes increasing to 25% organic fiber. Owner resists â€” it costs more. Committee organizes worker pressure, threatens to publicize the health impacts of synthetic fibers. Owner concedes.`
- **`Rung 5 (Iteration):`** `Over successive cycles, organic share grows to 50%, then 75%. At each step, the committee works with the federation to bring more regional farms into organic production, to adjust transport logistics, to line up clothing cooperatives as customers for the output.`
- **`Rung 6 (Full Social):`** `The fast-fashion market collapses (or is abandoned). The mill now sources 100% from regional farms, sells 100% to social customers. The capitalist owner, facing a mill that no longer serves their profit interests, sells or abandons. The committee assumes direct management.`

_`At every rung, failure was possible. If the organic farm had failed to deliver, the mill would have bought more synthetic fiber that month. If the clothing cooperatives had failed to buy, the mill would have sold more to fast-fashion. The market was the safety net that made bold transition possible.`_

**`Relation to Other Sections:`**

- **`PlanStore:`** `Stores both Current Plan (as observed reality) and Desired Plan (as proposed alternative). The gap between them is visible data.`
- **`Planner Process:`** `Used to test Desired Plans for feasibility. Autonomous plans run through the local planner. Social-Dependent plans run through federation planners, with their demands propagated upward.`
- **`Scope Committee:`** `The agent of transition. It manages the ladder, negotiates with the capitalist, reports to workers, brings demands to federation.`
- **`Social Plan Defense:`** `The militia protects the transition process. When the capitalist threatens violence, when scabs are brought in, when the state intervenes â€” the militia defends the committee's right to exist and to transition.`

## `Scope Committee Observer Procedure`

### `Unified Equations & Procedure`

#### `The Observer's Task`

The Scope Committee Observer's job is to **measure the gap** between current market operation and desired social operation, and to **calculate the transition parameters** for the next planning cycle.

The Observer tracks three flows:

1. **Physical flows** (what stuff moves in and out)
2. **Dollar flows** (what money moves in and out)
3. **Labor flows** (who works, for how long)

From these, the Observer computes the transition state and determines what to ask the social plan for.

---

#### `I. Core Measurements`

**`Physical Flow Fractions`**

Let:

| Variable                   | Definition                                                | How Measured                        |
| -------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------- |
| `total_inputs`             | Total quantity of all inputs received (in physical units) | Warehouse receipts, inventory logs  |
| `social_inputs`            | Quantity of inputs received from social plan sources      | Marked in receiving logs            |
| `total_outputs`            | Total quantity of all outputs shipped (in physical units) | Shipping logs, sales records        |
| `social_outputs`           | Quantity of outputs dispatched to named social recipients | Marked in shipping logs             |
| `confirmed_social_outputs` | Quantity confirmed received by named social recipients    | Receive EconomicEvents in PlanStore |

Then:

```
social_input_fraction     = social_inputs / total_inputs      (fraction of inputs sourced from social plan)
social_output_fraction    = social_outputs / total_outputs    (fraction of outputs dispatched to social plan)
confirmed_output_fraction = confirmed_social_outputs / total_outputs  (fraction confirmed received)
```

These are **physical ratios**, not dollar ratios. They measure real stuff, not prices. `social_output_fraction` and `confirmed_output_fraction` may differ while deliveries are pending confirmation or in quality dispute.

---

**`Dollar Flows`**

| Variable                 | Definition                                               | Source                             |
| ------------------------ | -------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------- |
| `market_revenue`         | Dollar revenue from market sales                         | Invoices to market customers       |
| `social_cash_revenue`    | Dollar revenue from social plan (if they pay cash)       | Invoices to social plan, transfers |
| `market_input_cost`      | Dollar cost of inputs bought on market                   | Invoices from market suppliers     |
| `social_input_cash_cost` | Dollar cost of inputs from social plan (if we pay cash)  | Invoices from social plan          |
| `dollar_wages`           | Total dollar wages paid                                  | Payroll records                    |
| `other_costs`            | Other dollar costs (taxes, fees, etc.)                   | Expense records                    |
| `subsidy_received`       | Dollars received from social plan as grants or subsidies | Transfer records                   |
| `contribution_sent`      | Dollars sent to social plan as contributions or fees     | Transfer records                   |

**Important:** The social plan may provide inputs without cash and may take outputs without cash. Kind flows are tracked in physical fractions only; only cash flows appear here.

Then:

```
net_dollar_flow = (market_revenue + social_cash_revenue + subsidy_received)
                - (market_input_cost + social_input_cash_cost + dollar_wages + other_costs + contribution_sent)
```

This must be **â‰¥ 0** for the scope to remain solvent in dollars.

---

**`Labor Flows`**

| Variable             | Definition                               | Source                  |
| -------------------- | ---------------------------------------- | ----------------------- |
| `hours_worked[i]`    | Total hours worked by worker i in period | Time cards, observation |
| `total_hours_worked` | `Î£ hours_worked[i]` across all workers   | Summation               |

**Note:** `hours_worked[i]` is the **pre-split total** â€” the worker's full hours for the period before any social/market fraction is applied. All social/market splits (`social_output_fraction`, `confirmed_output_fraction`) are applied as derived calculations on top of this number.

**Crucial correction:** While a worker's labor feels undivided on the factory floor, social validation reflects only the output that social recipients have actually confirmed receiving. Therefore, validated hours are derived from `confirmed_output_fraction`, not the dispatch fraction. The market plan funds the `(1 - social_output_fraction)` fraction; the social plan validates the `confirmed_output_fraction` fraction. `confirmed_output_fraction` may be less than `social_output_fraction` when deliveries are in dispute or quality has not been attested.

**`Social Validation Tracking`**

**`The Compensation Equations`**

For each worker, compensation comes in two forms:

```

```

The worker's `hours_worked[i]` (pre-split total) produced output that was split:

- Fraction `social_output_fraction` dispatched to social plan â†’ funds the claim pool
- Fraction `1 - social_output_fraction` went to market â†’ funds dollar wages

Therefore, the **fair relationship** should be:


**`Scope-Level Aggregates`**

As `communal_need_coverage[i]` grows, `effective_net_market_wage[i]` approaches `dollar_wage[i]` in discretionary character. The minimum viable dollar wage falls: workers can tolerate a lower `dollar_wage[i]` without real hardship because survival needs are met communally, not through market income.

The individual claims pool is a separate category â€” communal provision does not reduce it. Its character changes: as communal coverage expands, the claims pool shifts from a survival instrument to a discretionary one.

**`The substitution ratio`**

The key metric for worker perception:

```
fairness_ratio = [social_real_wage_per_hour Ã— confirmed_output_fraction]
              / [market_real_wage_per_hour  Ã— (1 - social_output_fraction)]
```

Expanded:

```
fairness_ratio = [average_claim_per_validated_hour Ã— claim_purchasing_power  Ã— confirmed_output_fraction]
              / [average_dollar_wage_per_hour      Ã— dollar_purchasing_power Ã— (1 - social_output_fraction)]
```

This is dimensionless: (SVC/hour Ã— goods/SVC Ã— fraction) / (dollars/hour Ã— goods/dollar Ã— fraction) = (goods/hour) / (goods/hour). Both prior formulations were incomplete â€” the per-hour rates alone omit purchasing power differences; the purchasing power terms alone omit the per-hour compensation rates. The correct formula requires both.

A gap between `social_output_fraction` and `confirmed_output_fraction` â€” unconfirmed or disputed deliveries â€” depresses the social numerator below expectation, making that gap immediately visible to workers.

If this ratio is **< 1**, the social portion pays worse in real goods. Workers will resist increasing `social_output_fraction` â€” or will pressure recipients to confirm outstanding deliveries.

If this ratio is **> 1**, the social portion pays better. Workers will resist decreasing `social_output_fraction` and may want to increase it faster.

If this ratio is **â‰ˆ 1**, compensation is balanced between the two systems.

---

**`The Communal Satisfaction Ratio`**

A companion metric to the substitution ratio, capturing the full social plan value against remaining market dependency:

```
total_social_value[i]       = (claim_capacity[i] Ã— claim_purchasing_power) + communal_need_coverage[i]

communal_satisfaction_ratio = total_social_value[i] / effective_net_market_wage[i]
```

Where the substitution ratio compares compensation streams per hour, the communal satisfaction ratio compares total social plan value â€” claims pool access plus communal provision â€” against what the market wage must actually cover. As `communal_need_coverage[i]` grows, `effective_net_market_wage[i]` shrinks even without any change in `social_output_fraction`: the communal satisfaction ratio rises from the expansion of communal provision alone.

The positive feedback loop:

1. More communal coverage â†’ lower `effective_net_market_wage[i]`
2. Lower minimum viable dollar wage â†’ less worker resistance to `social_output_fraction` increases
3. Faster transition â†’ greater social plan productive capacity
4. Greater capacity â†’ more communal provision â†’ back to step 1

This loop runs independently of the substitution ratio. A scope can hold a substitution ratio near 1 while its communal satisfaction ratio rises steadily â€” signaling that transition is deepening even where individual hourly compensation appears balanced.

#### `VI. Key Insight`

The Observer's math reveals that **the transition is constrained by two things**:

1. **`Dollar balance:`** Can we cover remaining dollar costs?
2. **`substitution ratio:`** Do workers get fair compensation in both dollars and claims?

Both must be satisfied at each step. The substitution ratio is where resistance will emerge. The Observer tracks it and shows when the transition is pushing compensation out of balance.

## `Social Plan Defense`

_`Scabs and private gunmen in factory plants are the basic nuclei of the fascist army. Strike pickets are the basic nuclei of the proletarian army. This is our point of departure.`_

**`Self-Defense Groups:`**

  - `Drill and acquaint members with the use of arms`

- **`Expansion:`**
  - `A new upsurge of the mass movement shall serve not only to increase the number of these units`
  - `But also to unite them according to neighborhoods, cities, regions`
  - `From isolated pickets to coordinated neighborhood defense`
  - `From neighborhood defense to citywide workers' militia`

**`Workers' Militia:`**

_`Advance the slogan of a workers' militia as the one serious guarantee for the inviolability of workers' organizations, meetings, and press.`_

- **`Composition:`**
  - `Drawn from the self-defense groups`

  - `Protect workers' meetings and assemblies`
  - `Secure workers' presses and communications`
  - `Guard liberated spaces and expropriated properties`
  - `Counter fascist organizing and violence`
  - `Provide disaster response and community defense`

- **`Command Structure:`**
  - `Commanders chosen by the militia units themselves`
  - `Drawn from the toilers, not from professional military caste`
  - `Trained in military schools established by workers' organizations`
  - `Immediately revocable by those who elected them`
  - `Bound by imperative mandate of the defending community`

**`Military Training:`**

- **`Content:`**
  - `Use of arms`
  - `Small unit tactics`

  - `First aid and disaster response`

  - `Training integrated with workplace and neighborhood â€” drill during breaks, evenings, weekends`
  - `Advanced training for militia officers`

**`Relation to the Standing Army:`**

_`The standing army is the armed instrument of the bourgeois state. The workers' militia is the armed instrument of the associated producers. The two cannot coexist indefinitely.`_

**`On the Character of Defense:`**
